International migration and modernization of rural origins

Bishal K. Chalise  ·  Mushfiq Mobarak
Nepal · 2001–2021

Research question

Does the FX-driven remittance windfall delivered through international migration modernise rural Nepal? We follow the demand side (household consumption, food security, asset accumulation), the supply side (firm formality, scale, productivity), and the realignment between them (employment composition, sectoral entry) using a Bartik-style shift-share instrument and three independent micro datasets.

Key data

  • Population census (2001 · 2011 · 2021) — 743 / 753 / 753 municipalities. 2001 round provides the baseline destination shares.
  • HRVS household panel (2016 · 2017 · 2018) — ≈ 6,000 HHs/wave; outcomes on remittance, food security, labour, enterprise.
  • NEC 2018 firm census, cross-section — 754 munis; outcomes on firm scale, formality, productivity, sectoral mix.
  • NEC firm panel — surviving firms by founding year 2001–2018, aggregated to muni × cohort.
  • Shock inputs — IMF FX panel (annual, 2000–2023); 2001 destination-country migrant shares; Comtrade 1995–2001; WDI 2001 dest-GDP.

Key results

All cells below: ssiv_index shock, mi controls (basic Khanna baseline = MI × year FE), lag0 timing. β reported per 1 SD of the standardised SSIV.

1. Census 2011→2021: stratified welfare gains, but amenity backsliding

Asset accumulation tracks the FX-driven remittance windfall: car ownership +0.085 pp** (mean 0.011), internet +0.760 pp*** (mean 0.129), higher-secondary attainment +0.167 pp*** (mean 0.152), tertiary attainment +0.083 pp** (mean 0.057). At the same time, basic-amenity shares fall in high-shock municipalities — lighting via electricity −1.07 pp***, piped drinking water −0.30 pp**, mean asset count −1.36**, mobile phones −0.54 pp** — consistent with sample-composition effects from migrant absences and selective in-migration into the receiving municipalities.

2. Households exit agriculture; labour-force participation falls

Agricultural workforce share −0.33 pp**, manufacturing share −0.07 pp**, construction share −0.12 pp** at the muni level. Female LFP −0.37 pp*** falls overall, with no offsetting rise in non-agricultural FLFP.

3. Modern-service and hospitality firm entry rises

NEC firm panel (founding-year × muni, 2001–2018): total surviving cohort firms +2,151** per muni, manufacturing entry +201**, hospitality +538**, modern services +5.8**. New firms are predominantly non-tradable.

4. No productive transformation in the firm cross-section

2018 NEC: formality index −0.37*** on a base of 0.51; share of micro firms (1 worker) +0.48 pp***; share of large firms (51+) −0.012** on a base of 0.002; mean revenue, median labour productivity, value-added per worker, and capital intensity all fall by 11–13% relative to their means. The cross-section says: high-shock municipalities have smaller, less formal, less productive firms.

5. HRVS HH panel: directionally consistent but imprecise

Household-level outcomes (remittance receipt, formal-channel remit, food-insecurity index, agricultural input use) point in the expected direction (more remit, more formal channels, less food insecurity) but are not statistically significant at the 5% level under household fixed effects. The 3-year HRVS panel (2016–2018) leaves limited within-HH identifying variation; results are informative but should be read alongside the muni-level census and NEC evidence.

6. The wedge, in one sentence

Remittance windfalls are an excellent welfare instrument and a poor industrial policy: Nepal's migration system delivers the first by accident; expecting the second is a category error.
