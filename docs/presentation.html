Background — Nepal & international migration

  • Personal remittances $\approx$ 22\% of GDP (World Bank, 2022–23) — the third-highest share globally after Tonga and Tajikistan.
  • About 3 million Nepalis are working abroad at any time, out of a working-age population of $\sim$20 million.
  • Main destinations: Malaysia, the GCC states (Qatar, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain), and India.
  • Outflows expanded sharply after 2001, driven by Nepal-to-Gulf labour contracts and the 1996–2006 Maoist insurgency.
  • At the muni level the destination mix is sticky: villages built corridors to specific destinations through network migration. The 2001 muni $\times$ destination matrix pins them down.

The identification puzzle & the shock we use

Why a naive regression won't do

Migration is endogenous. Places with more migrants may be poorer to begin with, or share shocks that drive both migration and welfare. To say anything causal we need exogenous variation in the income migrants send home, holding the migration network fixed.

The shock

Each destination's currency moves against the Nepali rupee over 2001–2023. Wages abroad convert to NPR; FX appreciation $=$ remittance windfall. FX movements are plausibly exogenous to any single Nepali municipality.

The shift-share trick

Combine destination-FX movements with the muni $\times$ destination shares fixed in 2001. Munis tied to appreciating destinations get a windfall; those tied to depreciating destinations do not.

Shift-share construction

Step 1 — baseline destination weights (2001 census)

\[ \omega_{md,0} \;=\; \frac{N_{md,\,2001}}{\mathrm{Pop}_{m,\,2001}}, \qquad \mathrm{mig\_int}_{m,0} \;=\; \sum_d \omega_{md,0}, \qquad s_{md,0} \;=\; \frac{\omega_{md,0}}{\mathrm{mig\_int}_{m,0}} \]

$N_{md,2001}$ = migrants from muni $m$ to destination $d$ in 2001. $s_{md,0}$ are destination shares (sum to 1 within muni).

Step 2 — destination-country shifter (annual)

\[ \mathrm{fxshock}_{dt} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{FX}_{dt}}{\mathrm{FX}_{d,\,2001}} \]

FX index of destination $d$'s currency vs NPR, indexed to 2001. Source: IMF FX panel, annual.

Step 3 — muni-year share-weighted shock

\[ \mathrm{fxshock}_{mt} \;=\; \sum_d s_{md,0}\,\mathrm{fxshock}_{dt} \]

Combines the shifter (destination FX, common across all Nepali munis) with the shares (each muni's 2001 destination mix). Standardised on the working sample before estimation.

Nepal migration / FX shock — Chalise & Mobarak Construction

Where the identifying variation comes from

  • Cross-muni: muni A sends 80\% of its migrants to Malaysia, muni B sends 80\% to the UAE. If MYR appreciates against NPR but AED depreciates, only A receives a positive shock.
  • Within-muni over time: each muni's destination mix is fixed at 2001, but FX shifters move year-by-year — generating panel variation in the treatment.
  • Heterogeneous slope. Main reduced form interacts the shock with each muni's 2001 baseline migration intensity: \[ \beta\,\bigl[\mathrm{fx}_{z,m,t}\,\cdot\,\log(\mathrm{mig\_int}_{z,m,0})\bigr] \] both z-scored. $\beta$ is the differential response per 1 SD of the treatment.
  • Identifying assumption (Borusyak–Hull–Jaravel, 2025): conditional on year FE, muni FE, year-interacted Khanna Block-A characteristics, and year $\times$ log-mig-intensity, FX shifters are quasi-randomly assigned across destinations.

Section 1 — first-stage evidence

Before any “migration income $\to$ welfare” claim, the instrument must move the things it is supposed to: outmigration at the muni level, remittance receipts at the household level, and the number of international migrants per household.

First-stage specification

\[ \begin{aligned} y_{it} \;=\;& \beta\,\bigl[\mathrm{fx}_z\!\cdot\!\log(\mathrm{mig\_int}_z)\bigr] \\ &+\; \sum_t \lambda_{1,t}\,\mathrm{mig\_int}_z\!\cdot\!\mathbf{1}\{t\} \;+\; \sum_t \lambda_{2,t}\,\mathrm{fx}_z\!\cdot\!\mathbf{1}\{t\} \\ &+\; \sum_{k,t} \delta_{k,t}\,X^A_{k,m(i),0}\!\cdot\!\mathbf{1}\{t\} \;+\; \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned} \]

$X^A$ = Khanna Block-A covariates: six destination-region migrant shares + 2001 destination-weighted GDP per capita, all interacted with year FE. Entity FE $\alpha_i$: muni (census) or HH (HRVS panel). SE clustered at municipality.

Nepal migration / FX shock — Chalise & Mobarak First stage — results

What this table says

  1. The shock raises outmigration at the muni level. The share of HHs with an absent member rises by $0.6\!-\!1.3$ pp per 1 SD of treatment, monotone in the threshold.
  2. Conditional on having a migrant, international remittance income rises by $\sim$$11\!-\!14\%$ in restricted samples ($k\geq 50$, $k\geq 100$).
  3. Conditional on having a migrant, the count of international migrants per HH rises by $\sim$$9\!-\!10\%$ in restricted samples.

All three margins are jointly identified by the same shift-share variation: more migration, more migrants per household, more rupees coming back.

Nepal migration / FX shock — Chalise & Mobarak First stage — interpretation

Reading the threshold pattern

Coefficients increase monotonically as the sample restricts to higher-baseline-migration municipalities ($k=0 \to 100$). Two coherent interpretations:

  • Mechanical. The shock-by-intensity interaction has more identifying variation precisely where intensity is non-trivial. Below $k\approx 25$ the interaction is mostly fitting noise.
  • Economic. Thin-migration munis don't have the destination network to capitalise on the FX shock; the strong response is concentrated in munis already on the migration frontier.

Both consistent with the network-multiplier logic of shift-share migration shocks.

One-sentence wrap

In rural Nepal, the FX-driven remittance windfall buys households a better toilet, a motorbike, and an extra year of schooling for their kids; it pulls men out of the village, leaves women holding the farm, delays marriage, and funds construction — but it doesn't build manufacturing, doesn't bring fixed broadband, and doesn't raise the local stock of educated adults, because the very people who would lift that stock are remitting from abroad.
