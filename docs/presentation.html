International migration
and modernisation of rural Nepal

Bishal K. Chalise  ·  Mushfiq Mobarak

Nepal · 2001 – 2021 · preliminary, do not circulate

Does the FX-driven remittance windfall delivered through international migration modernise rural Nepal? Evidence from a Bartik-style shift-share instrument applied across three independent Nepali micro-datasets.

Background — Nepal & international migration

  • One of the most remittance-dependent economies in the world. Personal remittances ≈ 22 % of GDP (World Bank, 2022–23), the third-highest share globally after Tonga and Tajikistan.
  • ~3 million Nepalis are working abroad in any given year — out of a working-age population of ~20 million.
  • Major destinations: Malaysia, the GCC states (Qatar, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain), and India.
  • Migration intensified after the 2001 baseline — the post-2001 expansion of Nepal-to-Gulf labour contracts and a domestic civil war (1996–2006) raised outflows sharply.
  • The destination mix is sticky at the muni level: villages established corridors to specific destinations through network migration; the 2001 muni × destination shares pin them down.

The identification puzzle. Migration is endogenous: places with more migrants may be poorer to begin with, or have shocks that drive both migration and welfare. We need exogenous variation in the income migrants send home, holding the migration network fixed.

The shock we use. Each destination's currency moves relative to the Nepali rupee over 2001–2023. Rs converts to wages → remittance amount sent home. FX is plausibly exogenous to any single Nepali municipality.

The shift-share trick. Combine destination-FX movements with the 2001 muni × destination shares. Munis with more migrants in appreciating destinations are exposed to a windfall; those tied to depreciating destinations are not.

Shift-share construction

Step 1 — baseline destination weights (2001 census)

\[ \omega_{md,0} \;=\; \frac{N_{md,\,2001}}{\mathrm{Pop}_{m,\,2001}} \qquad \mathrm{mig\_int}_{m,0} \;=\; \sum_d \omega_{md,0} \qquad s_{md,0} \;=\; \frac{\omega_{md,0}}{\mathrm{mig\_int}_{m,0}} \]

$N_{md,2001}$ = migrants from muni $m$ to destination $d$ in the 2001 census. $\omega$ is migrants per capita to each destination; $s_{md,0}$ are the destination shares of muni $m$ (sum to 1).

Step 2 — destination-country shifter (annual)

\[ \mathrm{fxshock}_{dt} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{FX}_{dt}}{\mathrm{FX}_{d,\,2001}} \]

FX index of destination $d$'s currency vs Nepali rupee, relative to 2001. Source: IMF FX panel, annual.

Step 3 — muni-year share-weighted shock

\[ \mathrm{fxshock}_{mt} \;=\; \sum_d s_{md,0}\,\mathrm{fxshock}_{dt} \]

Combines the shifter (destination FX movements, common across all Nepali munis sending to the same destination) with the shares (each muni's own 2001 destination mix). Treatment that goes into the regression is z-scored on the muni-year working sample.

Where the identifying variation comes from

  • Cross-muni: muni A sends 80 % of its migrants to Malaysia, muni B sends 80 % to the UAE. If MYR appreciates against NPR but AED depreciates, only muni A sees a positive shock.
  • Within-muni over time: each muni's destination mix is fixed at 2001, but the FX shifters move over 2001 → 2023, generating a time-varying treatment.
  • Heterogeneous slope — main reduced form interacts the share-weighted shock with each muni's 2001 baseline migration intensity: \[ \beta\,\bigl[\mathrm{fx}_{z,m,t}\;\cdot\;\log(\mathrm{mig\_int}_{z,m,0})\bigr] \] both z-scored. $\beta$ is the differential response per 1 SD of treatment.
  • Identifying assumption (Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel style): conditional on year FE, muni FE, year-interacted Khanna Block-A characteristics, and year × log-mig-intensity, FX shifters are quasi-randomly assigned across destinations.

Section 1 — first-stage evidence

Before any downstream "migration income → welfare" claim, we need to show that the instrument does move the things it is supposed to move: outmigration, remittance receipts, and the count of international migrants per household.

What we need the first stage to show

  1. The shock pushes more households into outmigration at the muni level.
  2. Households actually receive more remittance income.
  3. The number of international migrants per migrant household rises.

Without these, no downstream "migration income raises welfare" story is identifiable.

First-stage specification

\[ \begin{aligned} y_{it} \;=\;& \beta\,\bigl[\mathrm{fx}_z\cdot\log(\mathrm{mig\_int}_z)\bigr] \\ &+\; \textstyle\sum_t \lambda_{1,t}\,\mathrm{mig\_int}_z\,\mathbf{1}\{t\} \;+\; \sum_t \lambda_{2,t}\,\mathrm{fx}_z\,\mathbf{1}\{t\} \\ &+\; \textstyle\sum_{k,t} \delta_{k,t}\,X^A_{k,m(i),0}\,\mathbf{1}\{t\} \;+\; \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned} \]

$X^A$ = Khanna Block-A covariates (six destination-region shares + 2001 destination-weighted GDP per capita). Block B (origin baseline X) and Block C (trade SSIV) off in this run. Entity FE: muni (census) or HH (HRVS panel).

What this table says

  1. The shock raises outmigration at the muni level. The share of HHs with an absent member goes up 0.6–1.3 pp per 1 SD of treatment, monotone in the threshold.
  2. Conditional on having a migrant, international remittance income rises — by ~11–14% in restricted samples.
  3. Conditional on having a migrant, the count of international migrants per HH rises — by ~9–10% in restricted samples.

All three margins are jointly identified by the same shift-share variation: more migration, more migrants per HH, more rupees coming back.

Reading the threshold pattern

Coefficients increase monotonically as we restrict to higher-baseline-migration municipalities (k = 0 → 100). Two coherent interpretations:

  • Mechanical: the shock-by-intensity interaction has more identifying variation precisely where intensity is non-trivial; below k ≈ 25 the interaction is mostly fitting noise.
  • Economic: thin-migration munis don't have the destination network to capitalise on the FX shock; the strong response is concentrated in the munis already on the migration frontier.

Both consistent with the underlying network-cum-network-multiplier logic of shift-share migration shocks.

Where this goes next

  • Now that the first stage is established, the reduced-form outcomes are readable as IV-style responses.
  • Stable downstream patterns from the 48-spec grid:
    • Delayed marriage (−3.3 pp by 18, +0.14 yr mean age at marriage)
    • Sanitation revolution (+5.9 pp toilet, +7.1 pp ordinary toilet)
    • Asset upgrading to rural-mobility (motorbike + mobile up, car + fixed-internet down)
    • Male LFP down, feminisation of agriculture up, current schooling up
    • Construction boom; no manufacturing
  • Caveats: census absent_hh_share doesn't separate intl vs domestic absentees (next data task: rebuild from raw HH file). HRVS panel is short (3 years); HH FE absorbs most within-HH rupee variation, hence the muni-FE robustness alternative also reported in the back-up table.

One-sentence wrap

In rural Nepal, the FX-driven remittance windfall buys households a better toilet, a motorbike, and an extra year of schooling for their kids; it pulls men out of the village, leaves women holding the farm, delays marriage, and funds construction — but it doesn't build manufacturing, doesn't bring fixed broadband, and doesn't raise the local stock of educated adults because the very people who would lift that stock are remitting from abroad.
