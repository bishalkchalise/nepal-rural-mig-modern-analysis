International migration & rural Nepal

Presentation

Bishal K. Chalise  ·  Mushfiq Mobarak
Nepal · 2001 – 2021 · preliminary

Does the FX-driven remittance windfall delivered through international migration actually modernise rural Nepal? Evidence from a Bartik-style shift-share instrument across three independent Nepali micro-datasets.

Section 1 — First-stage evidence

Before any downstream "migration income raises welfare" claim, we need to show that the instrument does raise migration and remittance receipts. This section reports those first-stage regressions across four sample thresholds.

Sections to be added: identifying assumption · shift-share validity · headline reduced-form outcomes · stability across specifications · counterfactual magnitudes · policy.

Empirical strategy in one slide

  • Shock: destination-country FX index (relative to 2001) interacted with each municipality's 2001 share-weighted destination mix — a Bartik-style shift-share instrument.
  • Treatment of interest: \[ \beta\bigl[\mathrm{fx}_{z,m,t} \;\cdot\; \log(\mathrm{mig\_int}_{z,m,0})\bigr] \] heterogeneous slope: how strongly the FX shock bites where 2001 baseline migration intensity is high.
  • Threshold filter: repeat estimation on subsamples with $k=0/25/50/100$ baseline migrants — robustness to thin-migration municipalities.

Treatments z-scored on the muni-year working sample. SE clustered at municipality.

What we need the first stage to show

  1. The shock pushes more households into outmigration at the muni level.
  2. Households actually receive more remittance income.
  3. The number of international migrants per migrant household rises.

Without these, no downstream "migration income raises welfare" story is identifiable.

First-stage specification

\[ \begin{aligned} y_{it} \;=\;& \beta\,\bigl[\mathrm{fx}_z\cdot\log(\mathrm{mig\_int}_z)\bigr] \\ &+\; \textstyle\sum_t \lambda_{1,t}\,\mathrm{mig\_int}_z\,\mathbf{1}\{t\} \;+\; \sum_t \lambda_{2,t}\,\mathrm{fx}_z\,\mathbf{1}\{t\} \\ &+\; \textstyle\sum_{k,t} \delta_{k,t}\,X^A_{k,m(i),0}\,\mathbf{1}\{t\} \;+\; \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned} \]

$X^A$ = Khanna Block-A covariates (six destination-region shares + 2001 destination-weighted GDP per capita). Block B (origin baseline X) and Block C (trade SSIV) off in this run. Entity FE: muni (census) or HH (HRVS panel).

What this table says

  1. The shock raises outmigration at the muni level. The share of HHs with an absent member goes up 0.6–1.3 pp per 1 SD of treatment, monotone in the threshold.
  2. Conditional on having a migrant, international remittance income rises — by ~11–14% in restricted samples.
  3. Conditional on having a migrant, the count of international migrants per HH rises — by ~9–10% in restricted samples.

All three margins are jointly identified by the same shift-share variation: more migration, more migrants per HH, more rupees coming back.

Reading the threshold pattern

Coefficients increase monotonically as we restrict to higher-baseline-migration municipalities (k = 0 → 100). Two coherent interpretations:

  • Mechanical: the shock-by-intensity interaction has more identifying variation precisely where intensity is non-trivial; below k ≈ 25 the interaction is mostly fitting noise.
  • Economic: thin-migration munis don't have the destination network to capitalise on the FX shock; the strong response is concentrated in the munis already on the migration frontier.

Both consistent with the underlying network-cum-network-multiplier logic of shift-share migration shocks.

Where this goes next

  • Now that the first stage is established, the reduced-form outcomes are readable as IV-style responses.
  • Stable downstream patterns from the 48-spec grid:
    • Delayed marriage (−3.3 pp by 18, +0.14 yr mean age at marriage)
    • Sanitation revolution (+5.9 pp toilet, +7.1 pp ordinary toilet)
    • Asset upgrading to rural-mobility (motorbike + mobile up, car + fixed-internet down)
    • Male LFP down, feminisation of agriculture up, current schooling up
    • Construction boom; no manufacturing
  • Caveats: census absent_hh_share doesn't separate intl vs domestic absentees (next data task: rebuild from raw HH file). HRVS panel is short (3 years); HH FE absorbs most within-HH rupee variation, hence the muni-FE robustness alternative also reported in the back-up table.

One-sentence wrap

In rural Nepal, the FX-driven remittance windfall buys households a better toilet, a motorbike, and an extra year of schooling for their kids; it pulls men out of the village, leaves women holding the farm, delays marriage, and funds construction — but it doesn't build manufacturing, doesn't bring fixed broadband, and doesn't raise the local stock of educated adults because the very people who would lift that stock are remitting from abroad.
